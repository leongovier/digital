Case study · 2026 · checkRR

Replacing the 7-day document chase with a 5-minute applicant flow.

Most income-verification tools are built for underwriters. checkRR is built for the applicant who has to do the boring part — uploading three months of payslips on a Tuesday lunch break — and the broker who has to chase them when they don't.

01 — Problem

Document chases don't scale.

Talk to any specialist lender or broker about onboarding a self-employed applicant and you'll hear the same thing: three weeks of email tennis. The applicant uploads the wrong month, sends a screenshot instead of a PDF, password-protects the file, or just goes quiet.

Existing tools — Onfido, Plaid, the big credit bureaus — solve identity and connected-bank flows. None of them handle the messy middle: payslips, SA302s, and three months of bank statements that need parsing, deduplicating, and cross-checking against ONS spending benchmarks before an underwriter can sign anything off.

I'd watched two lenders try to build this in-house and shelve it. The pattern was always the same — a beautiful underwriter dashboard, no thought put into the customer-facing upload flow, and a 35% completion rate that nobody wanted to talk about.

02 — Research

Six conversations that shaped the product.

Before writing a line of code I sat with two underwriters, a broker, two compliance leads and one applicant who'd just been through a buy-to-let application. The same three frustrations came up again and again.

Insight 01

Applicants don't fail — instructions do

"I don't know what 'last 3 months' means — is that the last 3 calendar months or the last 3 pay cycles?" Every applicant we shadowed asked a variant of this. The form treats it as obvious. It isn't.

Insight 02

Underwriters re-key everything

Even when a file lands cleanly, an underwriter manually transcribes income figures into the OMS. Hours per case. They don't want a smarter inbox — they want a cleaner row in the system they already use.

Insight 03

Risk appetite is per-tenant, not universal

One lender's "borderline" is another's "reject". Any platform that ships hard-coded thresholds is dead on arrival in this market. The rules engine had to be the product.

03 — Process

From sketch to pilot in eleven weeks.

I worked back from the moment of friction — the applicant staring at a 4MB PDF on their phone — and forward from the moment of decision — the underwriter glancing at one screen to approve. Everything else had to disappear.

01

Map the chase

Six interviews, two ride-alongs. Mapped every email, document and decision point in a current 9-day average case lifecycle.

02

Find the wedge

"Send a magic link, get parsed payslips back" was the smallest thing that could prove the pitch. Everything else was V2.

03

Sketch on paper

Drew the applicant flow eleven times before writing a component. Mobile-first. Three steps, never four.

04

Build the brain

OCR + parsing + the affordability score weights. Tuned against 200 real (anonymised) payslip PDFs across 6 employers.

05

Wrap it in a dashboard

Underwriter view came last on purpose. Built around the three questions a UW actually asks: real? stable? enough?

06

Ship to three pilots

One specialist lender, one broker network, one bridging firm. Each got a tenant; each got the rules engine to tune.

04 — Applicant flow

If they don't finish, nothing else matters.

Most of the design effort went into the surface only the applicant ever sees. White-labelled to each lender's brand, mobile-first, and built so an applicant can resume from any device without losing progress.

The companion app uses SMS magic-link entry — no app to install, no account to create. The system coaches users through blurry photos, password-protected uploads and wrong-month files before they're submitted, so an underwriter never has to bounce a case for a fixable mistake.

Three steps. Five minutes. No back-and-forth.

The applicant gets an SMS, taps a link, verifies with a one-time code, and uploads payslips. The system reads them in 5–10 seconds, shows what it found, and asks for bank statements next.

  • SMS magic-link entry — no app to install, no account to create
  • Coaching for blurry, password-protected and wrong-month uploads
  • Real-time review summary before submit — no guessing what's been sent
  • Works in any modern browser — desktop, iOS, Android

05 — Underwriter dashboard

A single screen for the three real questions.

Underwriters don't want a spreadsheet — they want a verdict. The case page collapses everything into the three checks an experienced UW actually runs: is the income real, is it stable, and is there enough room for the loan.

Every figure is linked back to the document it came from. Every flag has a one-click expand to the underlying parse. Compliance loved it; underwriters stopped re-keying figures into Finova within a fortnight.

06 — Case intake

One form. Two paths. Live summary.

The biggest UX decision in the broker-facing form was branching inside the form rather than gating it. Employed and self-employed applicants need different documents — but you don't know which path applies until you've started typing names.

The right column is the receipt — every choice updates a live summary so the broker sees exactly what the applicant will be asked for, before sending the link.

/cases/new · employed
→Employed path · 6-month verification period default, payslips only, summary auto-counts to 12 expected documents.
/cases/new · self-employed
→Self-employed path · same form, switches to tax-year selector and SA302 upload. Customer-journey panel updates in lockstep.
07 — Admin & rules engine

The rules engine is the product.

Every lender wants a different DTI threshold, a different score weighting, a different risk appetite for bridging vs secured. If the platform shipped opinionated defaults, it'd be ripped out in 90 days.

Instead I built a configurable rules engine — read-only ONS benchmarks for the parts that should be uniform, editable thresholds for the parts that shouldn't, and visual feedback for every change so a compliance lead can see exactly what their tweak does to a notional case.

/admin/lending-criteria
→Lending criteria · pass/borderline/fail bands per loan type, with a delta against the secured baseline so risk teams can compare like-for-like.
/admin/system-settings
→ONS benchmarks · read-only by design. Compliance leads can see what we measure against without accidentally moving the floor.
/admin/feature-flags
→Feature flags · per-tenant kill switches. ID&V via Onfido, joint applications, SA302 upload — toggled, not coded.
08 — Integration & white-label

A sidecar, not a replacement.

Lenders don't want another system to learn — they want their existing origination platform, CRM and AI tools to feel smarter. checkRR connects to any API mature platform out of the box, and applies a tenant's brand colours from a single logo upload.

09 — Key decisions

What got cut, and why.

The version of checkRR that shipped looks nothing like the version I pitched. Every decision below was a tradeoff between "fits the demo" and "survives a real pilot".

Kept — what made the cut

  • Mobile-first applicant flow Mortgage applicants finish forms on the train, not at a desk.
  • Read-only ONS benchmarks The thing that makes Enhanced affordability defensible. Don't let tenants edit it.
  • Per-tenant rules engine The reason any of this is sellable. Without it, three lenders is the ceiling.
  • One-page case detail Underwriters scan, they don't drill. Top-of-page = the verdict.

Cut — what didn't survive contact

  • Open Banking auto-pull Faster on paper. Half of self-employed applicants don't connect; you still need the manual flow.
  • AI underwriter recommendations Pilot UWs hated being overruled by a black box. Moved to "AI summary" — descriptive, not prescriptive.
  • Native iOS app "App fatigue" came up in every applicant interview. Mobile web hits 95% of the same job.
  • In-app messaging Brokers prefer email. We surfaced status changes by email + dashboard — and shipped two months earlier.
10 — Outcomes

Three pilots. One sold. The rest are talking.

Eleven weeks from first wireframe to a working pilot at a specialist lender. The first pilot reported case turnaround dropping from 9 days to under 2 within the first month, with the bulk of the saving coming from the moments before an underwriter ever opened a case — applicants completing first time, in mobile, without a follow-up email.

The broader pattern, though, was qualitative: underwriters trusted the parsed figures enough to stop re-keying within a fortnight. Compliance signed off on the read-only ONS benchmarks without changes. Two of the three pilot tenants asked for a second cohort before the first had finished.

It's still early. The product is launching in Q2 2026, the pilot program is open, and the next thing to design is the broker-facing pipeline view — because the first thing every pilot lender did, after the first week, was ask for one.
