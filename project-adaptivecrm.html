Case study · 2026 · AdaptiveCRM

A CRM that flips the question from "find cars for customers" to "who wants this car?"

A full-stack CRM for a UK luxury car dealer, built in under two weeks using Claude and Claude Code as the primary workflow. Lead pipeline, stock inventory, AI-powered customer matching, and a margin-aware financial dashboard — designed, prototyped and shipped end-to-end by one designer.

01 — The brief

"Build us a CRM." That wasn't the real problem.

The brief came in as a standard CRM build — pipeline management, stock tracking, customer records. The kind of thing a developer could scaffold in a day and a designer could polish in a week.

But during the first scoping conversation a specific pain point reframed the whole project: "I find a car on AutoTrader. I think a customer wants it. I retype all the details into a spreadsheet, then I manually search through every customer to see who'd want it. Half the time I forget. Half the time I can't remember who said they wanted a grey SUV six months ago."

That's not a CRM problem. That's a reverse-search problem. And no CRM on the market solves it well for small independent dealers. The decision to build the product around that workflow — drop a screenshot, get ranked customers — was made in the first hour of the engagement. Everything else followed from it.

02 — The reframe

Find a customer for this car.

Every CRM on the market is built around the conventional direction: "I have a customer — find them a car." The real workflow for an active dealer is the inverse. Dealers browse AutoTrader constantly. They get WhatsApp messages from contacts about available stock. They walk around auction forecourts photographing vehicles. In each case, the question is always "which of my customers would want this?" — not "which car should I show this customer?"

Reframing the matching surface around this inversion was the single most important design decision in the product. It turned a secondary feature into the centrepiece, and it shaped the entire information architecture.

"This car hits every one of your criteria — make, budget, body type, mileage." A scored breakdown gives the salesperson something to open the call with. That's a better opening than a cold call.
03 — Domain research

Dealerships don't work like generic CRMs.

No formal user research phase — no shadowing, no interviews, no usability testing. The brief was build-first, with the bet that a working system in the dealer's hands would surface more actionable feedback than weeks of upfront discovery. Domain understanding came from the scoping conversation and prior industry knowledge. That trade-off is owned.

Three insights from the briefing shaped the architecture before the first page was designed.

Insight 01

The pipeline has eight stages, not four

New → Qualified → Matched → Appointment → Test drive → Negotiation → Deal agreed → Delivered. Generic CRM stages (Prospect / Lead / Opportunity / Closed) don't map to how a car sale progresses. Build a pipeline staff won't recognise and it'll be abandoned within a month.

Insight 02

Role hierarchy is a permission model, not a setting

Sales exec sees their own deals, no cost prices. Sales manager sees everything financial. Dealer principal sees margin and stock value. Get the model wrong and you either expose commercial data or block staff from doing their job.

Insight 03

Sourcing happens across three channels at once

AutoTrader pages, Facebook listings, WhatsApp forwards, auction forecourts. Any tool that only works with structured input (a URL, a form) fails for most real-world sourcing. The import layer had to handle unstructured text as a first-class input.

04 — Unified import

One modal. Three tabs. Same JSON.

The vehicle import tool could have been three features: an AutoTrader URL scraper, an image uploader, and a text field. Building them separately would have meant three UX patterns, three data pipelines, and three places a dealer goes depending on how they found the vehicle.

Instead: one modal, three tabs — URL · screenshot · text/chat. Claude extracts the same canonical JSON regardless of input. The matching engine and the data preview only had to be designed once. The dealer doesn't think about how they found the car. They just import it.

This was a product architecture decision, not a design decision. Every time a feature set started to diverge, the question was: is the underlying data model the same? If yes, one surface is almost always better than three.

URL

Paste an AutoTrader link

Page is parsed for make, model, mileage, price, photos. Falls back to the screenshot path if the page is JS-rendered or anti-bot protected.

Screenshot

Drop a photo

Vision extraction reads the listing or the car itself — number plate, badge, mileage, condition. Claude Haiku 4.5, roughly half a penny per lookup.

Text · chat

Paste a WhatsApp message

Free-form text from a contact, an auction note, or a forwarded listing. Same canonical JSON out. Same ranked matches.

05 — Build methodology

Design as a live widget. Build as a spec.

Every major page was prototyped as a live widget before a build prompt was written — dashboard, matches page, lead detail, stock wizard, match detail — designed, reviewed and iterated in conversation. Then a structured .md build prompt: what to build, the exact component structure, edge cases, acceptance criteria, effort estimate. Claude Code executed against the spec.

The gap between "designed" and "built" was measured in hours, not weeks.

06 — Permissions as design

A PermissionGate from the first prompt.

The role hierarchy was a first-class design constraint, not a developer afterthought. Cost prices, margin calculators, the financial dashboard, the tags admin — all gated from the first build prompt. Retrofitting permissions is one of the most common sources of bugs and security issues in internal tools. Designing them in prevented an entire class of problems.

Sales executives see their pipeline, their leads, their activity. Sales managers see the full team and full financials. Dealer principals see the stock value, the margin, the turnover. Same product, three reads of it.

07 — Decisions

The deferred list is as important as the shipped list.

A well-maintained "not now" list is product management. A growing "later" pile is technical debt. Every deferred item had a documented rationale and a clear path back into scope.

Kept — what made the cut

  • Unified import (URL / screenshot / text) One modal, three tabs, same JSON. The product architecture decision that made the whole thing work.
  • Match detail with scored breakdown Make 40, budget 20, body 15, mileage 15, tags 10. Gives the salesperson an opening line for the call.
  • Role-gated financial layer PermissionGate from prompt one. No retrofitting, no leaks.
  • Build prompt methodology Structured .md specs as PRD + architecture + impl guide. The single biggest reason this shipped in two weeks.

Cut — what didn't survive contact

  • Finance quote tool Out of scope early. The CRM is the wedge; finance gets its own surface later if it's still wanted.
  • AutoTrader URL scraper JS-rendered, anti-bot protected. The screenshot path covers the same job more reliably. Revisit if Partner API lands.
  • Native iOS app PWA covers mobile capture without a second codebase or App Store overhead. Install-to-home-screen is enough.
  • Lead-from-import Requires a customer-picker UX that needed its own design pass. Deferred, not abandoned.
08 — Outcomes

A working CRM, in the dealer's hands, in under two weeks.

A production-deployed full-stack CRM covering lead intake, stock management, customer matching, financial intelligence and social marketing. Live on Vercel. Built in under two weeks by one designer with Claude and Claude Code as the primary workflow.

The import workflow collapsed roughly ten minutes of manual searching down to under three per discovered vehicle — a 70% time reduction. Estimated five hours a week back to the sales team once embedded. Zero missed matches: the ranked list surfaces customers that manual memory-search would have missed entirely. AI extraction marginal cost: under half a penny per lookup.

The next surface is the mobile capture PWA — a /capture route for the phone-in-pocket workflow at auctions and forecourts. After that: competitor pricing tracking, match-to-sale analytics, and the AutoTrader Partner API once credentials land.
